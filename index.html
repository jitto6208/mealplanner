KKB

kitchen kebun baru

Born in a Kebun Baru kitchen, shared with the kampung. KKB is a living recipe collection and pantry tracker for Asian home cooks — built by a couple in Singapore whose idea of a good Friday night is a pot of something braised, a cold beer, and the smell of ginger hitting hot oil.

What started as a private list grew into something worth sharing. If you cook this way — a little Japanese here, a little Cantonese there, always something simmering on Sunday — this is for you.

How to use it

  • Recipes tab — browse, filter by cuisine or cook time, add your own dishes, and get the full recipe from Claude AI.
  • Pantry tab — track your staple ingredients. Tap a status badge to mark items as Low or Out. Hit "Top-up list" to see your shopping list at a glance.
  • Add anything — the collection is open to the whole kampung. Add your family's recipes and they'll appear for everyone.

The cuisines

The current collection spans Japanese (donburi, ramen, gyudon), Cantonese Chinese (steamed fish, clay pot rice), Thai (green curry, pad kra pao), Taiwanese (lu rou fan, beef noodles) and Vietnamese (lemongrass prawn vermicelli).

Tips for home cooks

1
Plan in cuisine clusters

The biggest gain is batching your decisions upfront. Think in "cuisine clusters" — group nights that share a protein or a sauce base. For example, if pork belly appears in both lu rou fan and chashu ramen, braise a larger batch on Sunday and use it across two nights. The flavour actually deepens by day two.

It also helps to keep a shortlist of 15–20 house recipes you both love and can rotate. Rather than discovering new dishes every week, you spend your decision-making energy only on the 2–3 exploratory slots per week. Less decision fatigue, more consistency.

2
Build a permanent pantry

Build a permanent checklist of the ~20 items that appear across almost every Asian dish — soy, mirin, sake, sesame oil, fish sauce, rice vinegar, oyster sauce, five spice, white pepper, sesame seeds, neutral oil, sugar, garlic, ginger. Once these are always stocked, your weekly shop shrinks to just proteins, fresh vegetables, and any specialty items for that week's dishes.

3
Batch cook on Sundays — the kampung way
  • Boil a batch of soft eggs and keep them in soy-mirin brine — they're ready to drop into ramen, donburi, or any rice bowl all week
  • Julienne a week's worth of ginger and store it in a small container in the fridge
  • Make double batches of any braising sauce (teriyaki, lu rou, or braising liquid) — they keep for a week and freeze well for a month
  • If doing a braise (pork belly, beef shank), cook it fully on Sunday. Monday's dinner is then just reheating and plating
4
